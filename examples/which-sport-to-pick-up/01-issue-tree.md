## Governing question
**Which sport should I pick up as a 30+ adult who'll actually stick with it for 5+ years?**

The unstated qualifier — "actually stick with it" — is doing load-bearing work. Picking the sport is easy. Picking one you'll still play in five years is the real problem.

## Issue tree

```
Which sport should I pick up (and stick with for 5+ years)?
│
├─ TIME COMMITMENT — Can I get to "competent" before motivation runs out?
│  ├─ Hours to feel competent enough to enjoy it (pickleball ~6, running ~10, tennis ~40)
│  ├─ Weekly hours to maintain a baseline skill (3–4 hrs)
│  └─ Scheduling flexibility — can I play solo, on demand, or do I need a partner + booked court?
│
├─ SUSTAINABILITY — Will my body let me play this for 5+ years?
│  ├─ Post-35 injury rate (tennis 2.3x pickleball; running compounds joint wear)
│  ├─ Repetitive stress profile (shoulder, knee, hip)
│  └─ Recovery time after a bad session (hours vs. days)
│
└─ SOCIAL FIT — Will the ecosystem keep me coming back?
   ├─ % of local courts/clubs that are beginner-friendly (pickleball 60%, tennis 18%)
   ├─ Do friends already play it (compounding effect — social density)
   └─ League / drop-in structure (easy to find a game vs. always organizing)
```

## Prioritization

Run the tests in this order. Each one can disqualify a sport before we look at the next.

1. **Sustainability first** — kills any sport you physically can't maintain. Anything with >2x injury risk vs. alternatives is out unless you accept the rehab cost.
2. **Time commitment second** — kills sports where the time-to-fun curve is longer than your motivation horizon. If "I have to grind 40 hours before this is enjoyable," most people quit at hour 12.
3. **Social fit third** — kills sports you can technically do but won't keep coming back to because there's no scene to plug into.

## Hypotheses to test with data

- **H1:** Time-to-competence varies by 5–7x across pickleball / running / tennis — and that's the single biggest predictor of stickiness.
- **H2:** Post-35 injury rate compounds quickly for sports with high lateral movement + reactive load (tennis, basketball) vs. linear/rhythmic (running, pickleball).
- **H3:** Social density (a friend already plays + local court availability) is a 3x multiplier on 5-year retention, dwarfing the choice of sport itself.

If all three hold, the recommendation falls out: **pickleball wins on H1 and H2, ties on H3 if any friend plays.**

## Common mistakes this tree is designed to avoid

- **Picking on intensity, not stickiness.** "Tennis is more athletic" is true and irrelevant — you only get the benefit if you keep showing up.
- **Ignoring the time-to-fun curve.** Sports with a 40-hour onboarding cost have a built-in attrition cliff.
- **Treating "I should exercise more" as the question.** That's a different problem (consistency), solved by lower friction, not by picking the right sport.
